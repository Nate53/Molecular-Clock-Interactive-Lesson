Introduction: Telling Time with DNA

How do biologists determine when two species shared a common ancestor, especially when the fossil record is incomplete? The answer lies in one of molecular biology's most powerful tools: the molecular clock.

The core idea is elegant: DNA accumulates mutations over time. If mutations accumulate at a roughly constant rate in certain regions of the genome, then the number of genetic differences between two species can serve as a "clock" that tells us how long ago they diverged from their last common ancestor.

A Brief History

1962 – The Idea Is Born
Chemist Linus Pauling and biologist Emile Zuckerkandl compared hemoglobin protein sequences across species. They noticed something remarkable: the number of amino acid differences between species correlated with how long ago the fossil record indicated they diverged. They proposed the concept of a molecular clock—the idea that molecular changes accumulate at a roughly constant rate and can be used to estimate evolutionary time.
1967 – Neutral Theory Provides the Mechanism
Japanese geneticist Motoo Kimura proposed the neutral theory of molecular evolution, arguing that most mutations at the molecular level are selectively neutral—neither helpful nor harmful. These neutral mutations drift through populations at a statistically predictable rate, providing the theoretical foundation for why a molecular clock could work.
1967 – Allan Wilson Shakes the Family Tree
New Zealand-born biochemist Allan Wilson and his student Vincent Sarich at UC Berkeley used molecular clock methods to study primate evolution. By comparing blood proteins (albumins) across primates, they estimated that humans and African apes diverged only about 5 million years ago—far more recently than the 15–25 million years that most anthropologists believed at the time. This was deeply controversial but ultimately confirmed by later fossil and DNA evidence.
1987 – "Mitochondrial Eve"
Wilson's lab later analyzed mitochondrial DNA from women across the globe. Using the molecular clock, they traced all living humans' maternal lineage back to a single woman in Africa roughly 200,000 years ago—a finding the press dubbed "Mitochondrial Eve." This work helped establish the Out-of-Africa model of human evolution.
Today
Modern molecular clocks use sophisticated statistical models, whole-genome data, and multiple calibration points from the fossil record. They remain one of the most important tools in evolutionary biology, especially for lineages with poor fossil records.
Allan Wilson (1934–1991)
Born in Ngaruawahia, New Zealand, Allan Wilson became one of the most influential molecular biologists of the 20th century. His use of molecular clock techniques to study primate evolution fundamentally changed our understanding of human origins. He was awarded a MacArthur Fellowship in 1986. Wilson died of leukemia in 1991 at age 56, but his methods and findings continue to shape evolutionary biology. His willingness to challenge established thinking—using molecular data when the anthropological community relied almost exclusively on fossils—is a powerful example of how new evidence can overturn long-held assumptions.

Why Does It Work?

The molecular clock depends on a critical concept: selective neutrality. Not all parts of the genome are equal.

Pseudogenes
"Broken" genes that no longer produce functional proteins. With almost no selective pressure, they accumulate mutations at a fast, steady rate—the "gold standard" clock.
Introns
Non-coding regions within genes that are spliced out before translation. Mostly neutral, their middle portions make a good fast-ticking clock.
Synonymous (Silent) Mutations
Changes in a codon that do NOT change the amino acid produced (due to redundancy in the genetic code). These are relatively neutral and provide a medium-speed clock.
Nonsynonymous Mutations
Changes in a codon that DO alter the amino acid. These are often removed by natural selection, so they accumulate very slowly—useful only for deep evolutionary time.
Key Principle: The most reliable molecular clocks use DNA regions that are selectively neutral. Neutral mutations are not "seen" by natural selection, so they accumulate at a roughly constant rate—like the steady ticking of a clock. Regions under strong selective pressure change at irregular rates and make poor clocks.

Assumptions and Limitations

Like any model, the molecular clock rests on assumptions that are not always perfectly met:

  • Constant mutation rate: The clock assumes mutations accumulate at a steady rate. In reality, rates can vary somewhat between lineages (e.g., organisms with shorter generation times may accumulate mutations faster).
  • Calibration required: Molecular clocks need at least one known divergence date (usually from the fossil record) to "set" the clock. The accuracy of the clock depends on the accuracy of this calibration.
  • Selective neutrality: The clock works best in regions free from natural selection. Strong selection can speed up or slow down changes in unpredictable ways.

Guided Lesson: How to Calculate a Molecular Clock

Let's walk through a molecular clock calculation step by step. We will use a realistic example to learn the method, and then you will apply it on your own in the Practice section.

Scenario: You are a molecular biologist studying the evolutionary relationship between humans and gorillas. You have sequenced a 1,000 base-pair (bp) segment of a homologous pseudogene from both species. After aligning the sequences, you count 16 nucleotide differences. A well-established fossil calibration point gives you a known mutation rate of 1 × 10-9 mutations per base pair per year.

The Formula

Divergence Time = Number of Differences ÷ (2 × Mutation Rate × Sequence Length)

Why divide by 2? Because mutations accumulate along both lineages after they split from the common ancestor. The total number of differences represents changes on both branches of the tree, so we divide by 2 to get the time back to the single common ancestor.

Problem 1: The Great Ape Family (Hominidae)

One of the most important questions in human evolution is: When did we last share a common ancestor with our closest living relatives?

Scenario: Your research team has sequenced a 2,000 bp segment of a homologous pseudogene from humans and four other living hominids. The calibrated mutation rate for this region is 1 × 10-9 mutations per bp per year. After aligning the sequences, you counted the following differences from the human sequence:
SpeciesDifferences from Human (out of 2,000 bp)
Chimpanzee24
Bonobo24
Gorilla32
Orangutan54

Calculate the estimated divergence time (time to last common ancestor) for each species from humans.

Based on your calculations, which species is our closest living relative? Explain what the chimp and bonobo results tell us.

Hint 1: Remember the formula: Divergence Time = Differences ÷ (2 × Rate × Length). Your rate is 1 × 10-9 and your length is 2,000 bp.
Hint 2: First calculate the denominator: 2 × (1 × 10-9) × 2,000 = 4 × 10-6 = 0.000004. Then divide each species' differences by that number.
Hint 3: For chimpanzee: 24 ÷ 0.000004 = 6,000,000 years. Notice that chimps and bonobos have the same number of differences from us. What does that tell you about when the human lineage split from the chimp/bonobo lineage?
Answers:
Chimpanzee: 24 ÷ (2 × 10-9 × 2,000) = 24 ÷ 0.000004 = 6,000,000 years ago (6 MYA)
Bonobo: 24 ÷ 0.000004 = 6,000,000 years ago (6 MYA)
Gorilla: 32 ÷ 0.000004 = 8,000,000 years ago (8 MYA)
Orangutan: 54 ÷ 0.000004 = 13,500,000 years ago (13.5 MYA)

What this tells us: Chimpanzees and bonobos are equally distant from humans (both ~6 MYA). This means the human lineage split from the chimpanzee-bonobo lineage about 6 MYA, and chimps and bonobos diverged from each other more recently (about 1–2 MYA). The gorilla lineage branched off earlier (~8 MYA), and orangutans are the most distantly related living great ape (~13.5 MYA). These molecular estimates align well with the fossil record.

Problem 2: Across the Animal Kingdom

Molecular clocks are not limited to primates. They can estimate divergence times across vastly different animal groups—even across different phyla.

Scenario: Researchers have sequenced a 500 bp segment of a highly conserved gene (the 18S ribosomal RNA gene) from a human and several other animal species. Because this gene is under strong selective pressure and changes slowly, its calibrated mutation rate is much lower: 5 × 10-10 mutations per bp per year (half the rate in Problem 1). The observed differences from the human sequence are:
SpeciesPhylumDifferences from Human (out of 500 bp)
ChickenChordata (Vertebrate)15
Fruit Fly (Drosophila)Arthropoda (Insect)30
Garden SnailMollusca27
Sea UrchinEchinodermata25

Calculate the estimated divergence time for each species from humans. Then rank them from most recent to most distant common ancestor.

Rank the species from most recently diverged to most distantly diverged from humans. Does the ranking surprise you? Why might the sea urchin (Echinodermata) be more closely related to humans than the fruit fly (Arthropoda)?

Hint 1: The formula is the same: Divergence Time = Differences ÷ (2 × Rate × Length). But note the different rate (5 × 10-10) and length (500 bp).
Hint 2: The denominator is: 2 × (5 × 10-10) × 500 = 5 × 10-7 = 0.0000005. Divide each species' differences by this value.
Hint 3: For chicken: 15 ÷ 0.0000005 = 30,000,000 years. Think about deuterostomes vs. protostomes. Echinoderms (sea urchins) are deuterostomes, like vertebrates, while insects and mollusks are protostomes. This is a major split in the animal family tree!
Answers:
Denominator = 2 × (5 × 10-10) × 500 = 5 × 10-7

Chicken: 15 ÷ 5 × 10-7 = 30,000,000 years ago (30 MYA) *
Sea Urchin: 25 ÷ 5 × 10-7 = 50,000,000 years ago (50 MYA) *
Garden Snail: 27 ÷ 5 × 10-7 = 54,000,000 years ago (54 MYA) *
Fruit Fly: 30 ÷ 5 × 10-7 = 60,000,000 years ago (60 MYA) *

* Note: These are simplified estimates for this exercise. Actual divergence times between these phyla (estimated using extensive genomic data and multiple calibration points) are much deeper—hundreds of millions of years. The gene and rate used here are hypothetical for pedagogical purposes, but the relative ranking is what matters for understanding the method.

Ranking (most recent to most distant): Chicken → Sea Urchin → Garden Snail → Fruit Fly

Why sea urchins are closer to humans than flies: This surprises many students! It comes down to a fundamental split in animal evolution. Humans and sea urchins are both deuterostomes (the blastopore becomes the anus during development), while fruit flies and snails are protostomes (the blastopore becomes the mouth). The deuterostome/protostome split occurred deep in evolutionary history, meaning sea urchins share a more recent common ancestor with us than insects do, despite looking nothing like us.
